SprayFight System

The Graffiti Battle System by TORB

Idea

Graffiti always grabs attention — with its scale, colors, and detail. But creating it can take a long time, and for viewers that process often feels slow: a piece can be worked on for hours or even all day.

At the same time there’s bombing — fast graffiti where the main point isn’t meticulous rendering but the act itself: “I was here.” It can be tags, throw-ups, simple letter styles, or characters.

People love watching how graffiti is made, and writers love to compete — who can do it better, faster, cleaner. Battles have existed for years, but most of the time they happen without a system — every city and country does it differently.

Goal of the system

  • make the process exciting to watch for the audience;
  • make it fun for writers to compete and win;
  • keep it dynamic, spectacular and easy to follow — while preserving the raw street spirit.

Format

16 writers take part. A bracket is formed and each battle is a single round — winners advance until the final. Every round has a new task and a strict time limit to simulate real street bombing conditions.

Rounds

Top 16

  • Time: 30 seconds
  • Task: write a random word (tag)
  • Goal: show style, speed, confidence

Top 8

  • Time: 1 minute
  • Task: random word as a throw-up or simple letter style
  • Color: one

Top 4

  • Time: 1 minute 30 seconds
  • Colors: two
  • Task: random word, more complex form
  • Judging: dynamics, balance, cleanliness

Final

  • Time: 3 minutes
  • Colors: up to four
  • Writers may swap names
  • Creativity, confidence and skill decide everything

(An optional 3rd-place battle can be added.)

Judging

A panel of 3 or 5 respected figures from different parts of the graffiti culture. The main thing is independence and diverse viewpoints. Judges evaluate:

  • letter style and form;
  • energy and confidence;
  • delivery and originality.

Production

If there’s no suitable wall nearby, a quick frame or swap panels are used. After each battle, helpers or the writers themselves cover the background so the next work is clear and visible. Perfect buffing is not required — the goal is simply to prep the surface for the next round.

This keeps the format fast, fresh and dynamic.

Why it works

I’ve been running graffiti battles for over five years and I see how this format truly pulls people into the culture. Viewers stick around — in fact, the crowd grows with each round.

It’s not just a contest — it’s a living process where art is born right in front of you. The format makes graffiti closer and more understandable to the public, and for the writers themselves it’s powerful motivation.

Many, after taking part or even just after watching, start to level up, develop their style, or pick up a can for the first time.

This system doesn’t only reveal the strongest — it helps grow a new generation of writers while keeping the street spirit and respect for the culture.
